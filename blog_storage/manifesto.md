MayaFlux
Indeterminate Form, Articulated Transformations

Preparing a piano for visual exploration does something precise and irreversible: it limits what the instrument can do sonically, or at minimum forces a constant negotiation between the two demands. The performer treads that line in real time. The tension is not incidental. It is the work. This is understood. What is less understood is that this constraint is not a property of creative practice in general. It is a property of physical systems. Digital computation does not have it. A matrix of numbers has no opinion about whether it is heard, seen, felt, or all three simultaneously at different rates. Nothing starves when you look at it. Nothing loses resolution when something else reads it. Route it to an audio subsystem at 48,000 samples per second and it becomes sound. View the same data at 24 frames per second and it becomes image. Copy it to a tactile reproducer, or run it ten times faster, or slower, or branch it into three simultaneous interpretations: the data does not care. It does not have a preferred domain. It never did. That was always a decision made by the tools, not by the material.

You noticed this. Or something like it. There were tools that excited briefly, that seemed to be pointing at this territory. They were. The vision was correct. What failed was not the ambition but the architecture: those tools carried the old categorical assumptions into the new space and called the result a paradigm shift. Audio here, video there, control signals routed through a separate nervous system, each domain with its own scheduling logic, its own buffer semantics, its own abstraction layer. The walls were repainted but they remained walls. If you stepped back from those tools it was not because the territory was wrong. It was because the territory was never actually entered.

What is here, then.

A number is a number. This sounds trivial and is not. In practice it means that the distinction between an audio sample, a pixel value, a control voltage, a positional coordinate, a probability weight is not a type distinction. It is a routing decision, made at the last possible moment, fully reversible, carrying no permanent consequence for the data itself. You can take a spectral analysis of a sound file and route its output directly to a GPU compute shader as geometry data, not as a special cross-domain feature requiring translation, but as the default condition, because there is nothing to translate. You can take the output of a physics simulation running at graphics rate and feed it into an audio filter's coefficient table at audio rate. You can run a grammar over a stream of any kind, a grammar that reads the stream's own statistical properties and decides in real time what transformation to apply, where the grammar itself is data and can be modified by another process running concurrently. None of this requires a special mode or a bridge or a translation layer. It requires only that you stop pretending the categories are real.

Time is the second property. In every tool you have worked with, time is infrastructure: a scheduler running underneath, a clock you respond to, a grid you align to or deliberately misalign with. You do not compose time. You work within it. In a system where coroutines are first-class computational objects, time becomes something different. A temporal structure is a value. You can pass it to a function, return it from one, compose two of them, inspect one's relationship to another. Sample-accurate timing and frame-accurate timing are not two separate systems requiring synchronisation: they are two different values of the same type, and you can build a structure that contains both and resolves the relationship between them mathematically rather than by convention. A process that needs to fire every 0.73 seconds in audio time while also aligning to the next visual frame boundary is not an edge case requiring workaround. It is a composition of two temporal objects. The algebra is the instrument.

The third property is recursion, and it is the one most thoroughly hidden by inherited tools. In physical systems, a process cannot take its own output as its next input without either a physical mechanism (a feedback loop, a resonant cavity, a delay line built from wire and space) or a significant engineering investment. Computation has no such constraint. Signal history is always available. A transformation that reads its own previous outputs and uses them to determine its next behavior is not an advanced technique: it is a for loop with memory. What this makes possible is not merely feedback effects in the audio sense. It is self-modifying processes, processes that change their own character over time based on what they have already done, processes that converge or diverge based on numerical properties you can inspect and manipulate directly. A filter whose coefficients are recalculated each buffer cycle from the statistical distribution of its own output. A visual process whose spatial grammar is updated by the spectral content of what it rendered three frames ago. These are not effects. They are behaviors. The instrument has behaviors, not just sounds.

There is a persistent assumption that engaging with these properties requires technical mastery as a precondition. It is worth being direct about this because it is wrong in a specific and diagnosable way. A violinist does not need to understand the physics of wood resonance to find a wolf tone. The wolf tone finds them, because the instrument permits the encounter. The barrier is not knowledge. It is whether the instrument has been designed to allow the negotiation at all. What has been consistently observed, across more than fifteen years of working with artists and designers at varying levels of technical background, is that the people who fail to engage with computational materiality are not the ones who lack programming knowledge. They are the ones who have been taught, implicitly or explicitly, that computation is a finished object you operate according to its rules rather than a material you negotiate with on your own terms. That teaching is a design choice embedded in tools. It is not a fact about computation. When the design changes, the people change. Participants with no prior programming experience, given access to a system that exposes its properties rather than concealing them, consistently find their way to the behaviors that matter to them. Not because the barriers were lowered, but because the barriers were revealed to have been artificial in the first place. Programming knowledge helps, the way physical dexterity helps a string player. It is an advantage, not a prerequisite. The instrument should not require it as a condition of entry.

What practice looks like here is harder to describe because it does not yet have a settled vocabulary. It looks something like: a composer who spends a month working with a single grammar definition, not tweaking parameters but understanding what the grammar's structure does to different kinds of input, finding the edge cases where it produces something unexpected, deliberately feeding it inputs it was not designed for. It looks like discovering that a process you designed for audio produces something visually interesting when routed differently, not as a happy accident but as an expected consequence of working with data that has no inherent domain. It looks like building a temporal structure, running it, and then modifying the structure while it runs, the way you might alter a prepared piano's preparations mid-performance but without the preparation consuming the sound. A year in this territory does not produce mastery of a tool. It produces fluency with a material. The distinction is the same one that makes the violin still interesting after four centuries. The system harbors more than any single practitioner exhausts. What is new is that the system is computational, the properties are mathematical, and the depth is not physical but logical. It does not wear out. It does not change with humidity. It scales with the quality of the questions you bring to it.

MayaFlux is an attempt to build the instrument that permits this negotiation. Not to describe it, not to point at it, but to build it: in C++, on Vulkan, with a live coding environment that compiles and runs changes within a single buffer cycle, with a type system where domain is a routing annotation rather than an identity, with time as a composable mathematical object, with grammar-defined transformation as a first-class operation. It is one year old. It is incomplete in the ways that any serious instrument is incomplete after one year. The architecture is in place. The depth is beginning to reveal itself. The invitation is not to be impressed by the architecture. It is to come and negotiate with the material.
